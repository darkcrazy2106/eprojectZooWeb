Classification

The scientific taxonomy of elephantids includes a rich record of fossil specimens, spanning millions of years, some of which persist until the end of the last ice age. Recently several species have become extinct. The discovery of new specimens and the proposed cladistics method has led to systematic revisions of them and related Proboscideans .

Elephants are informally classified as the family of elephants , or in paleontological contexts as elephants and mammoths. The common name elephant refers primarily to the extant taxon, the present elephant species, but may also refer to many extinct species, both within this and others. Other members of the family Elephantidae, especially members of the genus Mammuthus , are commonly known as mammoths .

The families branched off from a common ancestor of mastodons in the family Mammutidae . The classification is unstable and frequently revised.

Evolutionary history

Although the fossil evidence is uncertain, through a comparison of genes, scientists have discovered evidence that elephants and other Proboscideans share a common ancestor. distant from Sirenia (sea cow) and Hyracoidea (Demand). Along with the cetaceans ( Desmostylia ) and the order Embrithopoda they are thought to be extinct, belonging to the order Paenungulata ( Paenungulata).). In the ancient past, members of the various Daman families grew to large sizes, and the common ancestor of all three families is now thought to be an amphibious Daman. One theory is that these animals spent most of their time in the water, using their trunks as snorkels to breathe. Modern elephants have this ability and are known to swim that way for periods of up to six hours and distances of about 50 kilometers (31 miles). In the past, many species and genera have been discovered, including mammoths and pygmy elephants .
